Stay-at-Home Order Divorce Boom? Examining Data on Marital and Divorce Rates and the COVID-19 Pandemic.

With the onset of the COVID-19 Pandemic and subsequent government responses (business closures and stay-at-home orders), there was an expectation of a divorce boom as a result, with increased financial stressors and sudden close-contact with spouses. Divorce lawyers especially were predicting a wave of divorce filings in a post-COVID-19 world for previously discussed reasons. This project seeks to examine whether such a boom actually occurred in 2020, specifically using stay-at-home orders as a proxy for the pandemic.

State-wide Stay-at-Home orders were instituted at different times

As reports of COVID-19's spread worsened in early March 2019, the governments of many cities, counties, and states in the US made the decision to institute some variation of stay-at-home orders to stem the disease. For the purposes of this project, I only consider state-level status, as whether a state's governor had instituted a state-wide order or not. Thus, states such as Utah, who had counties and cities which did have stay-at-home orders, I denote as not having a State-wide order.

The visualization to the right illustrates the stay-at-home order status, categorizing states based on whether they instituted their order in a specific week. This data comes from the Wikipedia. States were split into categories

Here, we can see a lot of variation as to the timing of the stay-at-home orders, with a high majority of states who did undergo a stay-at-home order, did so within two weeks. There are a number of states, namely Utah, North Dakota, South Dakota, Iowa, Nebraska, Wyoming, Oklahoma and Arkansas, who by my definition did not have a stay-at-home order, and based on the divorce lawyer's hypothesis, should not see a change in divorce rates in comparison to the rest of the US.

However, almost all States, regardless of Stay-at-home order status, saw a decrease in Divorce rates

The black 45-degree line denotes a baseline where the 2019 and 2020 divorce rates are equal; above this line, 2020 rates are higher than 2019 and vice versa. State-level divorce rate data was acquired from The CDC National Vital Statistics Service.
